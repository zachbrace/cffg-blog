When Should You Claim Social Security?

Most people claim as soon as they can. The math often says don't. But math isn't the whole story. Here's how to think through a decision that could affect your income for decades.

Health and Family Longevity

If you have a serious health condition or a family history of shorter lifespans, claiming earlier may make sense. The math favors waiting, but math assumes you'll be around to collect.

On the other hand, if you're in strong health and your family tends to live into their late 80s or 90s, waiting typically wins. You'll collect that larger check for more years.

👫

Spousal Benefits

This is one of the most under-discussed pieces. The higher earner's decision matters more because the surviving spouse inherits the larger benefit.

If you're the higher earner and you wait until 70, you're protecting your spouse for the rest of their life. That larger benefit becomes their benefit when you're gone. For married couples, this can be the deciding factor.

"The right answer isn't about maximizing a spreadsheet. It's about what gives you confidence that your income will hold up for as long as you need it."

💰

Do You Need the Income Now?

If claiming early keeps you from drawing down retirement accounts during a market downturn, that can be a smart move. Social Security becomes a bridge, protecting your portfolio when you need it most.

If you don't need the income and can let it grow, waiting has a strong argument. But "can I wait" is different from "should I wait." It depends on your full picture.

📋

Taxes and Other Income

Social Security is partially taxable based on your combined income. Claiming while still working can push you into higher tax brackets and cause more of the benefit itself to be taxed.

Pensions, rental income, part-time work all affect the analysis. Someone with a strong pension has different math than someone whose Social Security will be their main income floor.

The Nuances Most Articles Skip

The Earnings Test
If you claim before Full Retirement Age and are still working, benefits can be temporarily reduced if your earnings exceed a threshold. Many people don't realize this and get surprised. The reduction isn't permanent, but it affects cash flow in those early years.
Survivor Benefits
Rules for widows and widowers differ from standard retirement benefits. You may be able to claim survivor benefits on a deceased spouse's record while letting your own benefit grow, then switch later. This is often overlooked.
Divorced Spouse Benefits
If you were married for ten years or more and are currently unmarried, you may be eligible for benefits based on your ex-spouse's record. Almost nobody knows this. It doesn't affect your ex's benefit at all.
COLA Compounds on a Bigger Base
Cost-of-living adjustments apply whether you claim early or late. But if you wait and lock in a bigger benefit, those percentage increases are applied to a larger number. Over time, this adds up.

This Decision Benefits from a Conversation

There's no one right answer. There's a right answer for your situation, given your health, your spouse, your other income, and your plans. This is exactly the kind of decision that benefits from talking it through with someone who understands your full picture. We're always happy to help.
